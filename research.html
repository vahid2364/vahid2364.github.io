Research Overview

The efficiency and operational life of sophisticated electronics (e.g., batteries, electronic chips, active wearable or implanted electronics) that enable our digital world are often determined by the materials selected and optimized for those applications. These innovations shape the world we know and allow the future we envision. However, transitioning a new material from discovery to practical use can take up to 20 years, and shortening this time is crucial to our economic competitiveness and prosperity.

My research addresses this grand challenge by leveraging high-throughput multi-physics and multi-scale methods that generate vast amounts of data, which are analyzed using Artificial Intelligence (AI). These methods build scientific robots capable of solving specific materials design problems, advancing the GREEN and BLUE economy.

Current Focus

My ongoing research centers on the accelerated design of materials, with a particular focus on high entropy alloys and thermoelectric materials. I aim to develop tools for solid-state inorganic materials that exhibit unique chemical, electronic, and structural instabilities, spanning domains such as:

  • Next-generation batteries (e.g., cobalt-free lithium and magnesium-based cells)
  • Electrochemical Random Access Memories (RAMs)
  • Thermoelectric materials for power generation (e.g., MgSnSiSe, SnSe)
  • Computational design of complex metal alloys

The exploration of novel materials in these areas involves diverse approaches, including searching crystallographic databases, modifying known materials, combining materials in heterostructures, and creating entirely new compounds.

Navigating rugged energy landscapes to determine phase stability in materials.

Research Phases

I have structured my research into three distinct phases: short-term, intermediate-term, and long-term. Each phase focuses on different aspects of materials design and discovery:

  1. Short-term: Developing high-throughput machine learning (ML)-based multiphysics computational tools for materials study.
  2. Intermediate-term: Establishing a research group to explore new directions in computational materials science using data science tools.
  3. Long-term: Becoming an independent researcher contributing significantly to the field, with a focus on mentoring the next generation of scientists and engineers.
